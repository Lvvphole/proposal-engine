# ADR-004: Services-as-Software Positioning

**Status:** Accepted
**Date:** 2024-06-01
**Author:** Emory

## Context

The proposal engine automates the 7–45 day post-quote window where residential contractors lose deals through slow follow-up. The question is how to package and price this capability.

## Decision

Position as **Services-as-Software** (SaS), not pure SaaS. Performance-priced at 8–10% of closed revenue from proposals generated by the system.

## Rationale

- **Aligned incentives:** We only get paid when the contractor closes. This eliminates the "another monthly subscription" objection and makes the ROI argument trivial — the contractor pays nothing if we don't generate results.
- **Higher LTV than flat SaaS:** A contractor closing $500K/year through our proposals at 8% generates $40K/year in revenue versus $200-500/month for a typical SaaS tool.
- **Moat through performance data:** Every closed proposal becomes training data that improves extraction accuracy and proposal quality. Competitors starting later face a cold-start problem.
- **Fragmented market fit:** Residential contractors are not software buyers. They don't want dashboards and settings. They want the quote turned into a proposal and sent. SaS wraps the technology in a service they already understand: "someone handles this for me."

## Go-to-Market

- **Pilot structure:** 3–5 roofers, free for 30 days, then 8% on closed proposals
- **Entry point:** Contractor Diagnostic Playbook identifies contractors losing >$50K/year to slow follow-up
- **Distribution:** Emory's existing Home Depot PSR relationships as warm leads (but the business is NOT anchored to HD — see commercial/CONTRACTOR_DIAGNOSTIC_PLAYBOOK.md)

## Technical Implications

- Must track proposal-to-close attribution to justify the performance fee
- Human review is non-negotiable — contractor's name is on the proposal
- Accuracy must be high enough that human review is a 30-second approval, not a rewrite
- The MCP server enables Claude Code integration for contractors who want a technical interface, but the primary UX is the review surface (approve/reject)

## Revenue Mechanics

Revenue recognition requires confirmed close. This means the system needs a feedback loop:

1. Proposal generated and approved by contractor
2. Proposal delivered to customer
3. Customer accepts (contractor confirms in review surface or via follow-up prompt)
4. Revenue recognized at agreed percentage

Step 3 is the bottleneck — we depend on the contractor reporting closes. The Weekly Tracking Dashboard (commercial/) is designed to make this reporting frictionless.

## Alternatives Rejected

- **Flat monthly SaaS:** Lower revenue per customer, misaligned incentives, harder to sell to non-technical buyers
- **Per-proposal fee:** Penalizes high-volume users, doesn't align with outcomes
- **Freemium + upsell:** Contractors who need this most won't engage with a free tier; they need the full service or nothing
